The Capital Asset Pricing Model

CAPM is a benchmark model that links an asset's expected return to its systematic risk: the risk that cannot be diversified away.

1. The main idea

The Capital Asset Pricing Model, usually called CAPM, says that investors should be compensated only for bearing systematic risk. This is the part of risk that moves with the whole market. Firm-specific risk can be diversified away by holding many assets, so CAPM says it should not earn an extra risk premium.

2. The CAPM equation

E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_i \bigl(E[R_m] - R_f\bigr)

Where:

SymbolMeaning
E[R_i]Expected return on asset i
R_fRisk-free rate
E[R_m]Expected return on the market portfolio
E[R_m] - R_fMarket risk premium
β_iBeta of the asset: its exposure to market risk

3. What beta means

Beta measures how sensitive an asset is to movements in the market. A stock with beta 1 tends to move like the market. A stock with beta above 1 is more exposed to market movements. A stock with beta below 1 is less exposed.

β = 0

No market-risk exposure. CAPM predicts the risk-free return.

β = 1

Same systematic risk as the market. CAPM predicts the market return.

β > 1

More systematic risk than the market. CAPM predicts a higher expected return.

The statistical definition is:

\beta_i = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(R_i, R_m)}{\operatorname{Var}(R_m)}

4. Worked example

Suppose:

  • Risk-free rate: R_f = 3%
  • Expected market return: E[R_m] = 9%
  • Asset beta: β_i = 1.5

Then:

E[R_i] = 3\% + 1.5(9\% - 3\%) = 12\%

CAPM predicts that this asset should have an expected return of 12%. The asset earns the risk-free rate plus 1.5 times the market risk premium.

5. The Security Market Line

CAPM can be drawn as a straight line relating beta to expected return. This is called the Security Market Line. The intercept is the risk-free rate, and the slope is the market risk premium.

\text{slope} = E[R_m] - R_f

Assets above the line look undervalued because their expected return is high relative to their beta. Assets below the line look overvalued because their expected return is low relative to their beta.

6. Assumptions

CAPM relies on strong assumptions, including:

  • Investors care only about mean and variance of returns.
  • Investors can borrow and lend at the risk-free rate.
  • All investors have the same beliefs about expected returns, variances, and covariances.
  • Markets are frictionless: no taxes, no transaction costs, and no short-sale constraints.
  • The market portfolio contains all risky assets.

7. Why CAPM matters

CAPM is used as a benchmark for estimating required returns, cost of equity, portfolio performance, and risk-adjusted pricing. Even though its assumptions are unrealistic, it remains a central model because it gives a clear prediction: only covariance with the market should be priced.

8. Limitations

Empirically, CAPM does not explain all return patterns. Size, value, momentum, profitability, and investment factors are examples of return patterns that motivated multifactor models such as the Fama-French models. Still, CAPM remains the cleanest baseline model for thinking about systematic risk and expected return.
